Ontologies provide a shared understanding of a domain. They are often used in the medical field, alongside electronic health records and clinical decision systems. These systems heavily manipulate personal data, which is sensitive.

Additionally, ontologies themselves can have an inherent private, and monetary, value, and need to be secured.

With ontologies growing in dimension, the industry is currently evolving towards the use of collaborative solutions and the outsourcing of heavy computations. This approach, albeit inevitable, presents new security concerns.

In this thesis, we investigate secure computation techniques, encryption protocols and semantic data storage systems with the finality of developing a practical and secure solution for the development of semantic data knowledge bases, which remain queriable after being encrypted.

Our main contributions are: (i) SPARQL-EVAL and SPARQL-EVAL-II, two \gls{SPARQL} query evaluation protocols, in the former we apply query rewriting techniques; (ii) SSE-TP, a triple pattern \gls{SSE} protocol, where encrypted \gls{RDF} data remains searchable via triple patterns; (iii) \gls{SEnTriQ}, an adaptation of the SPARQL-EVAL-II protocol that works with SSE-TP encrypted data.

The solution has been evaluated using the \gls{LUBM}, and the results indicate that it achieves high inference capabilities. The solution has been implemented with various levels of security, which offer a trade-off between security and performance. We argue that the solution remains practical to use, even at the highest level of security guaranties.

Ontologies, Knowledge Bases, SPARQL, Searchable Encryption, Multiplicative Blinding
\end{keywords}
